# Travelers, Friends, and the Price of Both

- **lore-id:** `npcs-and-friends`
- **Canon status:** Proposed (none of this has shipped; it is planned design the lore must be built for)
- **Sources:** none in game yet. The frost mechanics copy already says "any character or NPC", which is the one shipped crack of the door.

The designer's planned direction: Corpus is not empty. People and animals travel it, and the player's whole progression runs through them. Everything in this file is Proposed and written to be design-flexible, but the lore around it (factions, the ladder, claims) is already shaped to receive it.

## Travelers

Roaming people and animals move through the world on their own routes: peddlers, herders, hunters, families between somewheres, beasts that can be gentled. They can be met, bartered with, negotiated with, tamed, and, if you have ground worth offering, **invited to settle** on a claim.

Travelers are not quest dispensers and not scenery. In fiction they are what wanderers are: souls on the ladder, mostly on low rungs, mostly tired of climbing alone. An invitation to settled land is a real offer, and turning one down is a real judgment about your fences.

## The one who cannot fight

The planned protagonist can run and jump. That is the whole arsenal. What they have instead is the thing Corpus is worst at producing: people who trust them.

Progression is friendship made literal. The friends a player wins over do the things the player cannot: fight, chop wood, mine, haul, cook, guard. Mechanically the player plays as the friends they have befriended. In fiction, this is a small scandal. Mereonist doctrine measures a soul by what it takes (see [`systems/factions-and-faith.md`](../systems/factions-and-faith.md)), and here is someone rising on what they are given, freely, by people who like them. There is no comfortable Mereonist shelf for that. The Uncored do not entirely trust it either, since from the outside a settlement of devoted friends working someone else's claim looks familiar in a way they have a pamphlet about.

## Permanent death, the exception that needs an answer

Befriended NPCs die permanently, with some exceptions. This is the sharpest deliberate tension in the canon: Manus resurrects everything (see [`world/the-ladder.md`](../world/the-ladder.md)), and yet the people closest to the player can be lost for good.

**Proposed explanation, needs designer confirmation:** the bond does it. Manus's ladder returns each soul to its own rung to climb alone; that is the design, one climber per soul. A true bond fuses some of a soul's weight into the people it loves, which is exactly why friends make a non-combatant strong. But a soul that has given part of itself away no longer weighs enough, alone, for the ladder to catch. When a bonded friend dies, the part that was theirs returns to the climb somewhere; the person the player knew does not. Grief, in Corpus, is proof the friendship was real. The church finds this arrangement theologically tidy. Nobody who has buried a friend agrees.

This is flagged in [`meta/open-questions.md`](../meta/open-questions.md) as a genuine cosmological exception awaiting sign-off, along with the question of what the exceptions to permadeath are.

## Settling: campfire to Kingdom

Invited travelers settle on claimed land, and settlements grow in stages: **campfire, home, town**, onward step by step to **Kingdom**. Each stage supports more people; more people support stronger arrivals; settled NPCs open stores and crafts and whatever else ships. Population is power, and power is the ability to invite more. The full loop lives in [`systems/claims-and-realms.md`](../systems/claims-and-realms.md).

## Writing rules

- Travelers and settlers are souls on the ladder, never a separate lesser order of being. They have core counts, opinions about Mereonism, and reasons to want walls.
- Settled animals stay animals: no talking, onomatopoeia only, per [`species/wildlife.md`](wildlife.md).
- Do not write specific stores, professions, or settlement mechanics into lore until they ship; keep everything at the level of "what settling means" rather than "what the shop sells."
- Permadeath copy should never be cushioned. When lore touches a friend's death, it is a death. The rest of the setting jokes about dying; this is the one place the jokes stop.
