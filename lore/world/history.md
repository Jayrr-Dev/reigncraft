# History of Corpus

- **lore-id:** `world-history`
- **Canon status:** Proposed (Firelands ruins are Established; the Forgewright recast below is new and needs designer sign-off)
- **Sources:** `definingWorldPlazaFirelandsRuinBlueprintConstants.ts` (forge camps, portal shrines, obelisk circles, bastion settlements), `definingWorldPlazaFirelandsBiomeConstants.ts`

Corpus keeps poor records because most of its historians die mid-sentence and come back with other priorities. What exists is physical evidence (the Firelands ruins), church chronicle (Mereonist, so read with squinting), and word of mouth. This timeline is the bible's internal truth; in-game copy should still present the older parts as inference and rumor.

## Timeline

### Creation

Manus built the world as a contest and seeded it with the ladder: endless lives, ranked by power, scored in cores (see [`world/manus.md`](manus.md), [`world/the-ladder.md`](the-ladder.md)). He appointed the twelve Apostles to own and administer what living things need (see [`world/the-twelve-apostles.md`](the-twelve-apostles.md)). How long ago this was, nobody counts. The ladder does not run on a calendar.

### The Deep Past: the Forgewrights

Long before any current wanderer woke, a people lived in what is now the Firelands. Wanderers call them the **Forgewrights**, a name invented by whoever first looted their anvils.

The recast inside Manus canon, proposed as the single clean explanation: **the Forgewrights were the last serious challenge to the Apostles.** They were climbers, a whole civilization organized around the ascent, and the ruins read exactly like that:

- **Forge camps**: they worked metal with the land's own heat, arming and equipping at industrial pace.
- **Portal shrines**: they built gates, reaching for routes the Apostle of Transportation had not sold them.
- **Obelisk circles**: apparatus for working soul energy in bulk. What exactly they did with it is not recorded, and the circles do nothing observable now.
- **Bastion settlements**: they expected the answer to come from every direction, and fortified accordingly.

They got as far as no one since. Then their homeland burned.

### The Scorching

Something ended the Forgewrights and left the Firelands: scorched earth, ember vents, a permanent 62 degrees, volcanoes still active. In the recast, the Scorching is what the end of a failed climb looks like at civilization scale. Whether the killing blow was an Apostle's answer, their own apparatus getting away from them at the worst moment, or both at once, the bible deliberately does not say, and Mereonist chronicle is smug but vague ("the ladder holds; ask the ash").

What the recast does insist on: the Forgewrights are not gone. Nothing leaves the ladder. Whoever they were, they died, returned somewhere on some rung, and are climbing again or not, scattered and nameless like everyone else. The ruins are not a tomb. They are a scoreboard from a very good attempt.

The Scorching remains the setting's single historical catastrophe. Do not add a second one; scarcity keeps it heavy.

### The Long Quiet

Between the Scorching and the first wanderers: the world grew back. Forests spread, herds multiplied, wolf packs carved territories, and a few patient animals started climbing rungs of their own (see [`species/wildlife.md`](../species/wildlife.md) on Demon and Mythical individuals). The Apostles kept the books. The ash cooled.

### The Claim Age (now)

Wanderers began waking in Corpus: new souls thrown onto the ladder with empty hands and no memory of a before (see [`species/wanderers.md`](../species/wanderers.md)). They spread out from spawn, learned the biomes and the meats the hard way, and started claiming ground. Mereonism took shape around what the successful ones were already doing; the Uncored took shape around what it cost (see [`systems/factions-and-faith.md`](../systems/factions-and-faith.md)).

The Claim Age has no fixed start date on purpose. The multiplayer world is persistent and shared, so "how long have wanderers been here" stays vague enough to absorb real server history.

## Usage notes

- In-game text still attributes Deep Past claims to sources: "the ruins suggest", "the chronicle claims", "some wanderers say". Manus and the ladder are stated flat; the Forgewrights' story is not.
- "Forgewrights" is a wanderer nickname and copy can acknowledge that.
- New Firelands ruin props should slot into the four existing categories or extend the failed-climb reading, not introduce a new lost civilization.
- The recast (Forgewrights as a failed Apostle challenge) is Proposed and flagged in [`meta/open-questions.md`](../meta/open-questions.md) until the designer confirms it.
