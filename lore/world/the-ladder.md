# The Ladder

- **lore-id:** `the-ladder`
- **Canon status:** Proposed (cosmology); the mechanics it explains are Established (player respawn and death screens, wildlife respawn)
- **Sources:** death screen copy (YOU BURNED, YOU FROZE, YOU BLED OUT), `definingWildlifeSpeciesRegistry.ts` (wildlife respawn), `definingWildlifeNameTagConstants.ts` (size tiers up to Mythical)

"The ladder" is what everyone in Corpus calls the arrangement Manus built: endless lives, ranked by power, climbable by anyone with enough deaths in them. It is the setting's whole cosmology in one word. See [`world/manus.md`](manus.md) for the builder. Manus himself, in his Addresses, prefers to call it "the gift that keeps asking," which tells you most of what you need to know about both.

## Everything comes back

Everything that dies, Manus brings back. Monsters, bandits, kings, Apostles, wanderers, the wolf you killed for its meat. Death is a reset, not an exit. Nothing is allowed to leave the ladder, because the ladder only works if the weak can keep climbing until, some life, they are not weak anymore.

This is the canon explanation for two things players already see:

- **Wanderers respawn.** The death screen names how you died, never where you went, because you did not go anywhere. Manus put you back on your rung.
- **Wildlife respawns.** The herds refill and the packs return because animals ride the same ladder people do. An animal is not a lesser soul to Manus. It is a slower climber.

## The ugly consequences

The bible should treat these as everyday facts of the world, not twists:

**Your kills remember you.** An enemy you put down comes back, sometimes stronger, and sometimes it remembers whose hands did it. Wanderers tell stories about "the wolf I've killed five times," and the stories are true. A grudge in Corpus can outlast several bodies on each side. This is also why claims tend to hold: you cannot permanently remove an owner who respawns forever (see [`systems/claims-and-realms.md`](../systems/claims-and-realms.md)).

**Some climbers never catch up.** Endless lives is not the same as endless progress. Some souls die and return and die and return and stay at the bottom, forever. Manus does not care. The ladder is fair the way gravity is fair: same rules for everyone, no interest in outcomes. Asked about the ones who never rise, an Address answered: "They have not finished." The Uncored (see [`systems/factions-and-faith.md`](../systems/factions-and-faith.md)) consider this the whole indictment.

**The top of the ladder walks around.** The animals players see tagged Demon or Mythical are beings who climbed across many, many lives: killed, returned, grew, tried again, and kept some of the growth each time. A Mythical bear is not a freak of nature. It is a very old, very patient success story. (This replaces the old "soul saturation" folk theory, which is Deprecated; see [`species/wildlife.md`](../species/wildlife.md).)

## Merit and its receipts

Climbing has a currency. When anything dies, it drops the proof of its lives: its Soulcore (see [`world/soul-and-soulcore.md`](soul-and-soulcore.md)). Cores are how the ladder keeps score, and taking them off a corpse is how everyone, from the church to the vulture, participates in the arrangement Manus designed. To get stronger, something else has to become loot. Nobody in Corpus has found a way around that sentence, though the Uncored are trying.

## Writing rules for ladder content

- Death is routine but never trivial. Wanderers joke about dying the way sailors joke about storms. Keep the jokes; keep the weight under them.
- Return is explained now (Manus), but not decorated. No tunnels, gates, or afterlife imagery. You die, you are back. The blankness is the mercy and the horror at once.
- Rivalry with returning enemies is canon and encouraged in flavor copy, but individual named rivals are Proposed until a mechanic ships (see [`meta/open-questions.md`](../meta/open-questions.md)).
