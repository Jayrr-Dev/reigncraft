# The Twelve Apostles

- **lore-id:** `the-twelve-apostles`
- **Canon status:** Proposed (no Apostle content has shipped; boss mechanics do not exist yet)
- **Sources:** none in game yet

Manus appointed twelve Apostles to run Corpus for him: his hand-picked stewards, completely loyal, and worshipful without reservation. They are not gods. Never call them gods, in copy or in casual shorthand; Corpus has one god and the Apostles would be the first to say so, at length, while having you escorted out.

What they are is owners. Each Apostle governs one of the twelve things people need to live, and runs it the way Manus taught them: as a working part of one body, kept profitable in cores and answerable only upward. They gather, by custom, at Manus's Addresses, seated where everyone can see them agreeing. They do not rule people directly; they rule the stuff people cannot do without, which turns out to be the better lever. A wanderer can dodge a king. Nobody dodges lumber.

## Why they matter to a climber

To challenge Manus, a mortal must best all twelve Apostles first. Only then can they stand before the creator. The Apostles know this and take it seriously as a duty, not an insult: they are the ladder's final rungs, and screening challengers is part of the job. A mortal who beats all twelve is not committing rebellion. They are completing the paperwork for Manus's greatest wish.

Beating an Apostle grants nothing but the right to keep climbing. You do not inherit their holdings, their workers, or their ledgers. The wanderer who dreams of "winning the granaries" has misunderstood the contest. You conquer, and then you conquer more, and the world stays owned by whoever holds it.

## The Apostles, briefly

Each entry: who they are, how their devotion to Manus shows, and what challenging them might feel like. All of this is thematic sketch, written loose on purpose so boss design can land wherever it needs to.

### Willus Quill, Apostle of Land

The gentlest-mannered of the twelve and arguably the most powerful, since everything else stands on his domain. Quill grants land generously, on paper, with terms, and the terms are the trap. He calls every parcel in the world "held in trust for Manus" and himself merely the trustee, which is technically humble and practically absolute. Challenging Quill would mean fighting someone who owns the ground you are standing on, and the ground you retreat to, and the ground after that.

### Rockless Fellus, Apostle of Natural Resources

Quiet, methodical, and allergic to waste. Fellus does not want most of the ore, oil, and timber in the world. He wants all of it, tidily, and he got there by buying every rival's operation the day before they would have failed. His worship of Manus is bookkeeping: he tithes exactly, to the core, and considers a precise ledger a form of prayer. A challenge to Fellus would be a war of supply. He will not beat you; he will let you run out.

### Carnegus, Apostle of Raw Materials

Loud where Fellus is quiet. Carnegus turned mountains into girders and considers that the holiest act available to anyone, since Manus made a world of raw struggle and Carnegus makes it buildable. Famous for spectacular public gifts (bridges, halls, libraries of craft) funded by working his people to the bone; he sees no contradiction and will explain why at banquet length. Challenging him would mean surviving industry itself: heat, weight, and volume.

### Dominus, Apostle of Buildings

Every structure worth envying has his name on it somewhere, usually large. Dominus believes shelter is the first proof of merit ("animals sleep outside") and treats his towers as sermons. His devotion to Manus is competitive: he intends to be the most devout, measurably, and has commissioned the numbers to prove it. A challenge on his ground would be fought in his ground, room by gilded room, and he would narrate.

### Stanous Black, Apostle of Tools

The Apostle most wanderers would recognize on sight, because his mark is stamped on half the hammers in the world. Black is a craftsman first and an owner second, and among the twelve he is the closest thing to beloved: his tools work, and they last. His worship is simple. Manus made hands; Black makes what hands hold; the arrangement honors both. Challenging him would mean facing someone who knows every tool you carry better than you do, including its weak point.

### Herford, Apostle of Machinery

Herford's revelation was that anything done once can be done ten thousand times, identically, by a line of stations that never gets tired. He talks about rhythm the way priests talk about grace and considers an idle machine a small blasphemy. He admires Manus above all for the ladder's throughput: death, return, growth, repeat, the greatest production line ever built. A challenge would be fought against the line itself, tireless and interchangeable, while Herford times it.

### Vander, Apostle of Infrastructure

Roads, rails, ports, and everything that connects one place to another. Vander is blunt, physical, and proud of being the Apostle nobody can boycott: refuse his roads and enjoy the swamp. His devotion is territorial; he calls his network "the veins of Manus's body" and takes damage to it personally. Challenging Vander would mean getting to him, which is the fight. Every route belongs to him, and he decides what arrives.

### Amboser, Apostle of Energy

Keeper of light, heat, and current. Amboser is a relentless tinkerer with a showman's instinct and a long memory for credit disputes; every lit lamp in the world is, in his accounting, a small tribute to him and a large one to Manus. He calls energy "the breath of the ladder" since nothing climbs cold. A challenge would be fought in glare and darkness at his choosing, and he has had a very long time to prepare the switch.

### Leon Astronavis, Apostle of Transportation

The strangest of the twelve. Astronavis governs everything that moves people and goods, and openly says the ocean rim and the sky are not edges, just unshipped routes. Half his ventures fail spectacularly; the other half redraw the map. He worships Manus with ambition itself, on the theory that the boldest possible climb is the sincerest compliment to the ladder's architect. Challenging him would mean a fight that refuses to stay in one place, or possibly on the ground.

### Zeche Tore, Apostle of Technology and Knowledge

Owner of the patterns: craft secrets, formulas, the know-how that makes a tool worth holding. Tore rarely raises his voice and rarely needs to, since anything you know how to make, you likely learned from a pattern he licenses. Late in his tenure he has turned to grand public works of health and learning, which his critics call conscience and his admirers call the long game. His worship is archival: he is building a complete record of everything Manus's world has figured out. A challenge against Tore would be a fight against preparation. He has read your approach. He filed it years ago.

### Quacker Berg, Apostle of Data and Communication

The youngest-seeming Apostle and the hardest to look at directly, in the sense that he is usually looking at you first. Berg governs word, signal, and record: who said what, to whom, and what they were feeling at the time. He gives his services away free, which the other eleven found hilarious right up until he knew everything. His devotion to Manus is total surveillance framed as worship: the creator deserves a complete picture. A challenge would be fought against someone who has your correspondence, your habits, and your friends list.

### Japa Morgus, Apostle of Capital and Organization

The Apostle other Apostles borrow from, which tells you the ranking that actually matters. Morgus governs cores in motion: credit, contracts, the binding of promises to consequences. He has personally ended two panics and started at least one, and considers all three acts of stewardship. His worship of Manus is structural; he says the ladder is simply the truest contract ever written, with death as the enforcement clause. Challenging Morgus would mean discovering, mid-fight, exactly how much of your own strength was borrowed, and from whom.

## Writing rules for Apostle content

- Loyal, worshipful, not gods. Every Apostle genuinely reveres Manus; none schemes to replace him. Their flaws are the flaws of owners, not traitors.
- Loving parody, tongue in cheek, never naming the real-world echoes. If a joke only works by breaking the fourth wall, cut it.
- No shipped mechanics exist for any of this. Every gameplay implication here is Proposed and stays flexible until boss design happens.
- The Apostles rule things, not people. Copy about them should keep returning to their domains: the grain, the roads, the ledgers. That is where their menace lives.
