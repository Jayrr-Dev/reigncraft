# Factions and Faith

- **lore-id:** `factions-and-faith`
- **Canon status:** Proposed (no faction content has shipped)
- **Sources:** none in game yet

Corpus has one god, one dominant faith, and one rebellion against both. Nobody argues about whether Manus is real (see [`world/manus.md`](../world/manus.md)). The argument is about the ladder: whether the arrangement is mercy or a slaughterhouse with good bookkeeping. The bible does not pick a winner. Both sides get their best lines and their worst habits.

## Mereonism

The dominant faith. Its followers are **Mereons**, or in their own preferred phrasing, **the Worthy**.

The creed in one breath: power must be earned, the worthy rise, and unequal outcomes are proof of unequal merit. Mereonism holds that Manus built the fairest possible world, one where nothing is given and nothing is permanently lost, and that complaining about your rung is a confession about your climbing.

The founding text is a thick collection of sermons and ledger-parables called **The Weal of Wanderers**, attributed to the preacher **Adom the Sifter**, who taught that the ladder needs no shepherd because it herds itself. His most-quoted line: "No wanderer hauls cores for love of the village; and yet the village eats." Mereonist services quote him the way merchants quote prices, from memory and mid-transaction.

Mereonist doctrine names the same act differently depending on the pews:

- The church calls core-taking **merit**.
- Merchants call it **investment**.
- Nobles call it **ownership**.
- Wanderers call it **leveling up**.

The corpse on the ground probably has a different opinion, but the corpse will be back, which Mereonism considers a full answer to the objection.

Mereons venerate the Apostles as the ladder's appointed stewards (see [`world/the-twelve-apostles.md`](../world/the-twelve-apostles.md)) and Manus as the Quiet Hand, the maker who shapes everything and touches nothing. The faith's genius, and its tell, is that it asks nothing of the strong except to keep winning.

## The Uncored

The rebellion. Their creed in one breath: **"No soul should be measured by how many cores it owns."**

The Uncored hold that Manus built a slaughterhouse and called it merit; that every core is someone's spent life; and that every rich lord is, stripped of vocabulary, a successful grave robber. Their standard line about the great and the good: "Funny how every great man is standing on a mountain of dead people." Their jab at Mereonism itself: "The ladder is a lullaby. It sings the crushed to sleep with the word 'someday.'"

They are collective by conviction. Uncored camps pool cores in an open chest, feed whoever sits at the fire, and split spellwork costs by need rather than rank. Their sayings run in a shared cadence: "From the full hand to the empty one; from the empty one, nothing owed." Their oldest pamphlet, passed hand to hand and burned by Mereonist wardens on sight, opens: "A shadow walks Corpus, and it is ours. The owners have counted everything except us." Its closing line is the movement's whole recruitment pitch: "You who carry nothing: what can they take?"

Splinter sects add texture without changing the creed. **The Equal Flame** keep communal fires that anyone may cook at and no one may claim. **The Red Choir** are known for work songs with too many verses and, according to Mereonist complaints, exactly one subject.

Their insult for the player and everyone like them: **Corekeeper**. It lands because it is accurate. A wanderer who kills, loots, accumulates, and expands claims is precisely what the Uncored organized against, whatever the wanderer's intentions.

The Uncored are not written as saints. Their open chests attract informants and freeloaders in equal measure, their purges of "hoarders" have hit their own people, and their answer to "then how does anyone grow strong enough to challenge the Apostles" is, so far, silence.

## The shape of the conflict

The Worthy versus the Many. Ascension versus equality. Mereonism says let the worthy rise. The Uncored say no one rises alone; someone is always crushed underneath. Manus, who hears both, says: then climb anyway.

Neither side is purely right, and copy must not flatten either into villainy. Mereonism is honest about how the world works and blind to what that costs. The Uncored are honest about the cost and have no ladder of their own to offer. The player walks through the middle of this argument as its living example.

## How the satire is delivered

This is the load-bearing rule: the argument is felt through play, never stated at the player. The player naturally does what Mereonism celebrates (kill, loot cores, accumulate, expand claims), and the world comments quietly, in details:

- A poor village that cannot afford healing, because the cores left with whoever did the killing nearby.
- Workers who mine cores for an Apostle's operation all their lives and never own one.
- An Uncored campfire where dinner is free and someone eventually, politely, asks what you are carrying.

No sermons in shipped copy. No character explains the theme. If a detail needs a paragraph of framing to sting, find a sharper detail.

## Writing rules for faction content

- In-fiction prose never uses real-world economic or political vocabulary. The doctrines echo their sources in cadence and image only, and the echoes stay deniable. The reference key for writers lives in [`meta/style-guide.md`](../meta/style-guide.md).
- The Uncored are never called communists, in-world or in shipped copy. They are the Uncored, the Many, or their sect names.
- "Corekeeper" is an insult with a specific speaker (the Uncored, about accumulators). Do not let it drift into a neutral player title.
- Mereonist comedy is the Cucco register applied to money: deadpan, specific, and slightly alarming when read twice (see [`species/the-cucco.md`](../species/the-cucco.md)).
- Faction membership mechanics, reputation, and quests do not exist. Keep everything here at the level of culture and voice until systems ship.
